# How relevant are Mahabharata and Ramayana in contemporary times
In its present form the Ramayana consists of about 2400 shlokas and is divided into seven books. Mahabharata, which in its present form consists of over 100,000 slokas, equal to about eight times as much as the Iliad and Odyssey put together, is by far the longest poem known to literary history.1 In reality, one can speak of these texts as an epic and as a “poem” only in a very restricted sense. Indeed, the Mahabharata or the Ramayana is not at all a poetic product, but rather, an entire literature. In order to understand the dynamics involved in the building and evolution of these literatures, one needs to realize the context and background against which the divergent interpretations developed. We know that the literary activity in ancient India lay mostly in the hands of the priests, the Brahmins; and we have seen indeed how they have “Brahminised” the popular texts. Various forms (representation), in which the subjects are exposed to the literature, has to do with the appropriation of these texts, and in the way one looks at them, like stage adaption through rhapsodies and traditional dances, cartoons and soap operas, contemporaneity of these texts, bed-time stories, or reading the versions of the primary text, and so on. It is understandable that the meaning of the verses in the memory of rhapsodes gets shifted, that the text had to undergo often significant changes and that the reciters of the various regions made their own addition and elaboration to the structure of the texts.2 And in spite of the diverse elements of which it consists, this mighty works are considered by the Indians as a unitary compact work which has for its authors, the age old Vyasa(Mahabharata) and Valmiki(Ramayana). The historiography of Indian scholar (the secondary sources of the epics) was affected by the notions of western academics, thus leading to production and reproduction of the product of an autochthonous history.
‘The fashionable injunction to study reality as a text should be supplemented by the awareness that no text can be understood without a reference to extratextual realities.’3 At least 1500 years ago, these epics were already as or at least similar to- what it is today in our manuscripts. Mahabharata and Ramayana, besides complimenting each other, cuts across markers of time, colour, race, language and gender, the markers which have continued to haunt human beings over millenniums. The Ramayana and Mahabharata cannot be studied by the binaries of good-bad, right-wrong, sacred/profane, as these dichotomies are not the structure of telling stories. What is possible though, while interpreting these epics, is to see them through the lenses of the relationship between karma and dharma, while understanding the difference between dhamma and dharma, in the modern context. 
Law of karma is relation between action and its fruit, which ensures that morally determinate action does not disappear into nothingness but eventually meets its due retribution, the good with happiness, the bad with suffering.4 But Devdutt Patnaik explains the concept of Maya where, in Sanatan Dharma, the karma is not how it is understood.5 The biblical reference of “you reap what you sow” is different from the Sanatan Dharma. Apart from the traditional law, Dharma is believed to be the act of respecting and helping the helpless, without considering the consequences of their actions. The past, which neglects of the value of precise and detailed comparison, has left us with nothing but general impressions, no more reliable for their frequent repetition, on questions of what can be believed and what can be assumed. 
The Dhamma and Dharma as dichotomies, not necessarily in the light of religious practices, namely Buddhism and Brahminism, but with respect to their ideologies, which Devdutt Patnaik elaborates in his lecture, plotting them in the context of the epics. Dhamma Pada broadly formulates to a practical spirituality, guidelines to or simply a way of right living, and “one who will follow the Dhammapada will taste the bliss of emancipation.”6 Dhamma connotes to a way of life outside worldly, a life of a hermit, a sanyasi. Dharma, on the contrary, are social and economic obligation of a householder, a sansari which he/she has to perform, involuntarily. The framework of “Hindu” society is the emphasis on dharma (duties) and, judging by the law books, access to right is limited to the privileged class.7 Mahabharata and Ramayana are epics which focuses on Dharma stapana, while rejecting, quite strongly, the ideas of Dhamma.8 The Dharma can be understood only if one does not resort to advocating the righteousness of one’s action. This can be traced back to Ramayana, where Ravana, driven by his ‘Dharma’, inimically believes Surpanka, and abducts Sita from the forest. Ravana, a Vedic scholar, a devotee of Shiva, is killed by Rama for kidnapping Sita. Sita, after all the ‘Ramayana’, is abandoned by Rama, back into the forest, for she is seen as ‘impure’.9 In an episode of Mahabharata, Bhishma, the son of King Shantanu and River goddess Ganga, for his father`s happiness (to marry Satyavati), sacrifices his throne (performing Dharma ulanghana), and vows to carry on a life of Brahmachari and serve to the ruler of Hastinapur. It was due to this decision of his that the kingdom sees various downfalls, ultimately leading to the war between cousins.10
An obstinate refusal to question of right/wrong, good/bad, is not just on the basis of subjectivity, or merely relativity. Let us take a case. A woman, who is supposed to get married to a man, calls off the wedding a week before the wedding as she realizes that her education will be compromised if she gets married. The man, on the other hand, feels betrayed by the decision. He realizes the amount of money spent and the loss of his family integrity. He thus files a complaint report in the court. The court case goes on for years ruining the woman`s career. This case can be “judged” differently by different people, bringing all kinds of pre-existing views. 

Conclusion:
While one can still contest on the righteousness of the Sita and evil deeds of Rama, one can say Karna was right, and Krishna was wrong, citing different narratives, on the basis of various questions, and still not get the perception and presumptions of the epics. A simple way to investigate the historical text, as dynamic as these, and still make sense of them in the modern context, is to see them through the lens of Karma (actions) and Dharma (consequences).
